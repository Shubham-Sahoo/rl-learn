# Module 5: Advanced Policy Optimization

> Understanding natural gradients, trust regions, and entropy-regularized RL is foundational for RLHF — the same algorithmic ideas govern how language models are fine-tuned with human feedback.

---

## 1. Intuition

When we run gradient descent on a neural network, a small step in **parameter space** (Euclidean distance in $\theta$) does not correspond to a small step in **policy space** (how much the distribution over actions actually changes). Two parameter vectors that are numerically close can produce wildly different action distributions — and vice versa.

This is the core problem: vanilla gradient descent treats all parameter directions equally, but the policy landscape is curved. A gradient step that looks small by the Euclidean norm in $\theta$-space may catastrophically change what the policy does, or it may move barely at all in the space of distributions.

The key insight of natural gradient / TRPO / SAC is to measure progress in **policy space** rather than parameter space. We want updates that are meaningful in terms of *how the policy behaves*, not just how the numbers in $\theta$ change.

*(Why? Because RL objectives care about trajectories generated by the policy, not about Euclidean distance between parameter vectors.)*

---

## 2. Fisher Information Matrix

The **Fisher Information Matrix (FIM)** quantifies the local curvature of the statistical manifold — how sensitive the policy distribution is to parameter changes.

$$F_{ij}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_\pi\!\left[\frac{\partial \log\pi_\theta}{\partial\theta_i}\frac{\partial\log\pi_\theta}{\partial\theta_j}\right]$$

**Geometric interpretation:** $F$ is the **Riemannian metric** on the statistical manifold of distributions. It defines an inner product on the tangent space at $\theta$:

$$\langle u, v \rangle_F = u^\top F(\theta)\, v$$

This means the "distance" between two nearby policies $\pi_\theta$ and $\pi_{\theta + d\theta}$ — measured in KL divergence — is:

$$\text{KL}(\pi_\theta \| \pi_{\theta+d\theta}) \approx \frac{1}{2} d\theta^\top F(\theta)\, d\theta$$

*(Why? Because KL divergence is the natural notion of distance between distributions, not the Euclidean distance between their parameters.)*

The FIM is also equal to the **negative expected Hessian of the log-likelihood**:

$$F(\theta) = -\mathbb{E}_\pi\!\left[\nabla^2_\theta \log\pi_\theta(a|s)\right]$$

This connects it directly to second-order optimization. Unlike the Hessian of the reward, the FIM is always **positive semi-definite** (it's an outer product of gradients), making it well-behaved for optimization.

---

## 3. Natural Gradient

The **natural gradient** rescales the standard gradient by the inverse FIM:

$$\tilde{\nabla}J = F^{-1}\nabla J$$

**KL-geometry interpretation:** The natural gradient is the direction of steepest ascent in $J$ *subject to a fixed KL divergence budget* $\delta$. Formally, it solves:

$$\arg\max_{d\theta} \nabla J^\top d\theta \quad \text{s.t.} \quad d\theta^\top F\, d\theta \leq \delta$$

whose solution is $d\theta^* \propto F^{-1} \nabla J$.

*(Why? Because in Euclidean space steepest ascent is just $\nabla J$, but in a curved (Riemannian) space we must account for the metric $F$ when measuring step size.)*

**Why natural gradient takes consistent steps in policy space:** Multiplying by $F^{-1}$ removes the effect of the parameterization. Regardless of whether we use logit parameterization or softmax parameterization, the natural gradient points in the same direction in the space of distributions. Vanilla gradient descent does not have this property — it is parameterization-dependent.

**Practical challenge:** $F$ is $|\theta| \times |\theta|$ and cannot be materialized for large networks. TRPO solves this by using conjugate gradient (CG) to compute $F^{-1}g$ without ever forming $F$ explicitly.

---

## 4. TRPO

**Trust Region Policy Optimization** makes the natural gradient approach practical by solving a constrained optimization problem each update:

$$\max_\theta \hat{\mathbb{E}}_t\!\left[\frac{\pi_\theta(a_t|s_t)}{\pi_{\theta_\text{old}}(a_t|s_t)}\hat{A}_t\right] \quad \text{s.t.}\quad \hat{\mathbb{E}}_t[\text{KL}(\pi_{\theta_\text{old}}\|\pi_\theta)] \leq \delta$$

The objective is the **importance-weighted surrogate** — it estimates how much better the new policy is relative to the old one, using samples collected under $\pi_{\theta_\text{old}}$. The constraint ensures the new policy doesn't stray too far from the old one (staying within the "trust region").

*(Why the surrogate? Because we want to improve $J(\theta)$ but can only collect data from $\pi_{\theta_\text{old}}$. The importance ratio $\pi_\theta / \pi_{\theta_\text{old}}$ corrects for this distribution mismatch.)*

**Conjugate gradient for $F^{-1}g$ without materializing $F$:**

TRPO solves $Fx = g$ using the conjugate gradient algorithm, which only requires the product $Fv$ for arbitrary vectors $v$. This **Fisher-vector product** (FVP) is computed via **double backpropagation**:

1. Compute KL divergence $D_\text{KL}(\pi_{\theta_\text{old}} \| \pi_\theta)$ over the batch.
2. Compute $k = \nabla_\theta D_\text{KL}$ (first backprop).
3. Compute $k^\top v$ (dot product with query vector).
4. Compute $\nabla_\theta (k^\top v)$ (second backprop) → this is $Fv$.

*(Why double backprop? The FIM is $\mathbb{E}[(\nabla \log \pi)(\nabla \log \pi)^\top]$, and $Fv = \mathbb{E}[(\nabla \log \pi)(\nabla \log \pi)^\top v]$. The gradient of a gradient-dot-vector gives exactly this without forming the matrix.)*

After CG solves for $x = F^{-1}g$, TRPO does a **line search** (backtracking) to find the largest step $\alpha$ such that the KL constraint is satisfied.

## 5. Maximum Entropy RL

**MaxEnt RL** augments the reward objective with a policy entropy bonus:

$$J(\pi) = \mathbb{E}_\tau\!\left[\sum_t\gamma^t\bigl(R(s_t,a_t) + \alpha H(\pi(\cdot|s_t))\bigr)\right]$$

where $H(\pi(\cdot|s)) = -\mathbb{E}_{a \sim \pi}[\log \pi(a|s)]$ is the entropy and $\alpha > 0$ is the **temperature** parameter.

**Why entropy regularization?**
- **Exploration:** High entropy policies explore more, avoiding premature convergence to suboptimal deterministic policies.
- **Robustness:** The optimal MaxEnt policy is softer — it retains probability on suboptimal actions proportional to $\exp(Q(s,a)/\alpha)$. This leads to policies that are more robust to reward misspecification.
- **Multiple modes:** When the reward landscape has multiple equally good solutions, MaxEnt finds a policy that hedges between them rather than committing to one arbitrarily.

*(Why? Because a deterministic policy that achieves reward $R^*$ is one solution, but there may be many near-optimal stochastic policies. Entropy regularization prefers the most uncertain one among the near-optimal set, which is desirable for generalization.)*

The **soft Bellman equation** for MaxEnt RL is:

$$Q^\text{soft}(s,a) = r + \gamma \mathbb{E}_{s'}[V^\text{soft}(s')]$$
$$V^\text{soft}(s) = \alpha \log \int \exp\!\left(Q^\text{soft}(s,a)/\alpha\right) da$$

The soft value function is the log-sum-exp (softmax) of Q-values — hence "soft."

## 6. SAC — Soft Actor-Critic

SAC is a practical off-policy MaxEnt RL algorithm for continuous action spaces. It combines twin Q-networks, reparameterization, and automatic temperature tuning.

### Reparameterization trick

Instead of sampling $a \sim \pi_\phi(\cdot|s)$ directly (which blocks gradient flow), SAC uses:

$$a = \tanh(\mu_\phi(s) + \sigma_\phi(s)\odot\varepsilon),\quad\varepsilon\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)$$

*(Why tanh? Tanh squashes actions to $(-1, 1)$, matching many continuous control environments. The reparameterization $a = f(\varepsilon; \phi, s)$ makes $a$ a deterministic function of $\varepsilon$ and $\phi$, so we can backprop through the sample.)*

### Log-prob with squashing correction

The change-of-variables formula gives the log-probability of the squashed action:

$$\log\pi(a|s) = \log\mathcal{N}(\tilde{a};\mu,\sigma) - \sum_i \log(1-a_i^2+\varepsilon)$$

where $\tilde{a} = \tanh^{-1}(a)$ is the pre-squash action and $\varepsilon$ is a small constant for numerical stability.

*(Why the correction term? The Jacobian of $\tanh$ is $1 - \tanh^2(\tilde{a}) = 1 - a^2$, so the log-determinant of the Jacobian is $\sum_i \log(1-a_i^2)$. This must be subtracted from the Gaussian log-prob to get the correct log-density of the squashed distribution.)*

### Q-target (twin critics)

$$y = r + \gamma\!\left(\min_{i=1,2}Q_{\theta_i^-}(s',\tilde{a}') - \alpha\log\pi(\tilde{a}'|s')\right)$$

where $\tilde{a}' \sim \pi_\phi(\cdot|s')$ is a freshly sampled next action, and $Q_{\theta_i^-}$ are **target networks** (slowly updated copies).

*(Why twin critics? Single Q-networks tend to overestimate Q-values (a form of optimism bias). By taking the minimum of two independent Q-estimates, we get a conservative value estimate that reduces overestimation and stabilizes training.)*

*(Why target networks? Without them, the bootstrap target $y$ depends on the same parameters being updated, creating a moving target problem that causes training instability.)*

### Critic loss

$$\mathcal{L}_Q(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{(s,a,r,s') \sim \mathcal{D}}\!\left[\frac{1}{2}\!\left(Q_\theta(s,a) - y\right)^2\right]$$

### Actor loss

$$\mathcal{L}_\pi(\phi) = \mathbb{E}_{s \sim \mathcal{D},\, \varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}}\!\left[\alpha\log\pi_\phi(\tilde{a}|s) - \min_{i=1,2}Q_{\theta_i}(s, \tilde{a})\right]$$

*(Why minimize this? Maximizing $\mathbb{E}[Q - \alpha \log\pi]$ is equivalent to maximizing the expected soft Q-value, which is the MaxEnt objective. Negating gives a minimization loss.)*

### Automatic temperature tuning

Instead of fixing $\alpha$, SAC learns it to maintain a target entropy $\bar{H} = -|\mathcal{A}|$ (one bit of uncertainty per action dimension):

$$\mathcal{L}(\alpha) = \mathbb{E}[-\alpha\log\pi(a|s) - \alpha\bar{H}]$$

*(Why? We want the policy to explore enough (entropy $\geq \bar{H}$) without being too random. If entropy falls below $\bar{H}$, $\mathcal{L}(\alpha)$ increases $\alpha$, pushing the policy to be more exploratory. If entropy exceeds $\bar{H}$, $\alpha$ decreases. This is a Lagrange multiplier for the entropy constraint.)*

## 7. MARL — Multi-Agent RL

### Setting

In **multi-agent RL (MARL)**, multiple agents $i \in \{1, \ldots, N\}$ act simultaneously in a shared environment. Each agent has its own observation $o^i_t$, action $a^i_t$, and reward $r^i_t$. The joint action $\mathbf{a}_t = (a^1_t, \ldots, a^N_t)$ determines state transitions.

### Cooperative vs competitive

- **Cooperative:** All agents share the same reward signal. The goal is to maximize collective return. Example: multi-robot coordination.
- **Competitive (zero-sum):** One agent's gain is another's loss. Example: game-playing AI.
- **Mixed:** Some agents cooperate, some compete. Example: team sports, traffic.

### Non-stationarity challenge

From any single agent's perspective, the environment is **non-stationary** — the other agents are also learning and changing their policies. Standard single-agent convergence guarantees break down because the effective environment keeps shifting.

*(Why? Because $\pi^j$ appears in the transition dynamics from agent $i$'s point of view. As $\pi^j$ changes, the Q-function for agent $i$ changes even without any change in $\theta^i$.)*

### Partial observability

In most realistic MARL settings, each agent can only observe a portion of the full state. Agent $i$ receives $o^i_t \subset s_t$. This is called a **Decentralized Partially Observable MDP (Dec-POMDP)**.

### Centralized Training / Decentralized Execution (CTDE)

CTDE is the dominant paradigm for cooperative MARL:

- **During training:** A centralized critic has access to the full global state $s_t$ and all agents' actions $\mathbf{a}_t$. This eliminates the non-stationarity problem because the full joint state is observed.
- **During execution:** Each agent's policy only uses its local observation $o^i_t$. No communication with other agents is needed.

*(Why CTDE? Training with full information is easier and more stable. Execution with local information is required for deployment in decentralized systems where communication is unavailable or unreliable.)*

### QMIX

**QMIX** (Rashid et al. 2018) learns individual Q-functions $Q^i(o^i, a^i)$ per agent, then combines them via a **mixing network** into a joint $Q_\text{tot}(\mathbf{s}, \mathbf{a})$ with a **monotonicity constraint**:

$$\frac{\partial Q_\text{tot}}{\partial Q^i} \geq 0 \quad \forall i$$

This constraint ensures that each agent's individually optimal action is also jointly optimal (the **IGM — Individual Global Max** property). The mixing network's weights are conditioned on the global state but are constrained non-negative (via absolute values), enforcing monotonicity.

### MADDPG

**MADDPG** (Lowe et al. 2017) extends DDPG to MARL using CTDE. Each agent $i$ has:
- A **decentralized actor** $\pi^i_{\phi^i}(o^i)$ — uses only local observations at execution time.
- A **centralized critic** $Q^i_{\theta^i}(s, a^1, \ldots, a^N)$ — uses global state and all actions during training.

This allows the critic to account for other agents' behavior, stabilizing training despite non-stationarity.

## 8. Failure Modes

### TRPO failure modes

- **Expensive conjugate gradient solve:** CG requires many FVP (Fisher-vector product) computations per update. Each FVP requires two backward passes through the policy network. For large networks (e.g., transformers), this is prohibitively expensive.
- **Trust region too small:** If $\delta$ is set too conservatively, TRPO barely moves per update, converging slowly.
- **Trust region too large:** If $\delta$ is too large, the quadratic approximation to the constraint breaks down, and updates may violate the true KL constraint.
- **Sample inefficiency:** TRPO is on-policy — collected rollouts are discarded after each update. This makes it expensive in wall-clock time for environments with slow simulators.

*(PPO addresses most of these by replacing the hard KL constraint with a clipped surrogate objective, which is much cheaper to optimize.)*

### SAC failure modes

- **Reward scale sensitivity:** The temperature $\alpha$ and target entropy $\bar{H}$ are relative to the reward scale. If rewards are poorly scaled (e.g., $r \in \{-1000, 1000\}$), automatic temperature tuning may oscillate or converge slowly. Always normalize rewards.
- **Twin critics oversmoothing:** Taking the minimum of two Q-estimates introduces a **pessimism bias** that can be too conservative in some environments, underestimating the value of exploration. This is particularly problematic in sparse-reward settings where even slightly suboptimal actions need positive credit.
- **Hyperparameter sensitivity:** SAC's performance depends on learning rates for actor, critic, and temperature, as well as target network update rate $\tau$. These interact in non-obvious ways.
- **Soft policy convergence:** In theory, soft policies converge to the MaxEnt optimum. In practice, with function approximation, the entropy term can cause the policy to remain overly stochastic even when a near-deterministic policy would achieve higher reward.

## 9. Research Bridge

### Temperature $\alpha$ ↔ LLM sampling temperature

The entropy regularization temperature $\alpha$ in MaxEnt RL has a direct analogue in language model sampling. When an LLM generates text with temperature $T$:

$$P(a|s) \propto \exp(\text{logit}(a) / T)$$

This is exactly the soft policy $\pi(a|s) \propto \exp(Q(s,a) / \alpha)$ from MaxEnt RL. High temperature = more exploration/diversity; low temperature = more exploitation/determinism. In RLHF, the KL penalty term in the objective ($-\beta \text{KL}(\pi \| \pi_\text{ref})$) plays exactly the role of $\alpha H(\pi)$ — it controls how much the policy is allowed to diverge from the reference model.

### SAC twin critics ↔ reward model ensemble

The pessimistic Q-estimate $\min(Q_1, Q_2)$ in SAC is a specific instance of **uncertainty-aware value estimation**. In RLHF, reward model ensembles serve an analogous purpose: using $\min$ (or mean minus std) of multiple reward models prevents the policy from overoptimizing a single reward model's blind spots. Both techniques address the same root cause — a single function approximator will have systematic errors that a greedy policy will exploit.

### FIM ↔ Fisher-Rao distance in RLHF

The Fisher Information Matrix defines the **Fisher-Rao metric** — the natural Riemannian metric on the space of probability distributions. In RLHF fine-tuning, the KL divergence between the fine-tuned model and the reference model $\text{KL}(\pi_\theta \| \pi_\text{ref})$ serves as a constraint. For small deviations, this KL divergence approximates the squared Fisher-Rao distance: $\text{KL}(\pi_\theta \| \pi_{\theta_0}) \approx \frac{1}{2}(\theta - \theta_0)^\top F(\theta_0)(\theta - \theta_0)$. The natural gradient is thus directly relevant to understanding why KL-constrained RLHF training behaves differently from unconstrained fine-tuning.
